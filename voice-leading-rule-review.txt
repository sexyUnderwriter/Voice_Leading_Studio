1) Leading Tone Unresolved (33)
-------------------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- In tonal counterpoint, the leading tone (scale degree 7) has strong tendency to resolve up by semitone to tonic.
- In G major, that means F# is expected to move to G in accented or cadential contexts.
- The analyzer flags cases where F# holds, descends, or leaps elsewhere instead of resolving to G.

Representative report examples:
- m.4 beat 1.00 (Alto): F# -> E
- m.7 beat 2.50 (Soprano): F# -> A
- m.21 beat 4.00 (Alto): F# -> C#

Why these may be musical anyway:
- In fugue texture, passing, neighbor, or sequence-driven lines may defer or avoid immediate tendency-tone resolution.
- Inner voices can sometimes move to chordal tones other than tonic if harmony is clear and voice leading remains smooth.

Correction options:
- Preferred: move F# to G in the next note in the same voice, especially at cadences and downbeats.
- If harmonic function requires otherwise, delay but eventually complete the tendency resolution by step.
- Revoice surrounding parts so F# becomes non-structural (passing tone) with weaker accent.


2) Voice Crossing (20)
----------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- A lower voice moves above a higher voice (or vice versa), blurring registral hierarchy.
- The revised detector already ignores unison landings and direct pitch swaps, so these are more likely true crossings.

Representative report examples:
- m.9 beat 1.00: Soprano crosses Alto; Alto crosses Tenor
- m.16 beat 2.00: Alto crosses Tenor
- m.38 beat 1.00: Alto crosses Tenor

Why these may be musical anyway:
- Imitative writing can tolerate brief crossings when subjects overlap.
- Some crossings are texturally intentional for color or registral exchange.

Correction options:
- Keep each line within its normal register envelope (S > A > T > B).
- When two voices converge, route one by contrary step while the other holds or moves less.
- Re-octavate one note in the inner voice to restore vertical order without changing harmony.


3) Parallel Unisons (107)
-------------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- Two voices move together in repeated unisons over successive attacks (unison -> unison).
- This merges lines and reduces contrapuntal independence.

Representative report examples:
- m.6 beat 2.00: Soprano & Tenor unison -> unison
- Many sustained chains in Bass & Viola through middle sections

Why these may be musical anyway:
- If one line is a reinforcement doubling (especially in arrangements), repeated unisons can be deliberate.
- With Viola removed, many of these may disappear in the practical version.

Correction options:
- Convert one member of the pair to thirds, sixths, or oblique motion.
- Keep occasional unison arrivals but avoid repeated consecutive unison motion.
- Offset one voice rhythmically by suspending or anticipating its next pitch.


4) Parallel Octaves (13)
------------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- Two voices form a perfect octave on one attack and another perfect octave on the next while moving in similar motion.
- This weakens perceived independence similarly to parallel unisons.

Representative report examples:
- m.7 beat 3.00: Soprano & Bass (and Soprano & Viola)
- m.21 beat 1.00: Alto & Bass (and Alto & Viola)

Why these may be musical anyway:
- In thicker ensemble textures, occasional octave reinforcement can be stylistically tolerated.
- But repeated structural octaves are generally treated as an error in strict species-style counterpoint.

Correction options:
- Alter one voice by step to arrive at 10th/6th instead of 8ve.
- Use contrary motion into the second sonority.
- Reassign one voice note to neighboring chord tone.


5) Parallel Perfect Fifths (17)
-------------------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- Consecutive perfect fifths in similar motion between two parts.
- In strict common-practice pedagogy, this is one of the primary independence violations.

Representative report examples:
- m.11 beat 3.00: Soprano & Tenor
- m.21 beat 2.00: Soprano & Bass (and with Viola)
- m.32 beat 2.00: Alto & Bass

Why these may be musical anyway:
- In sequential passages, temporary fifth chains may arise from pattern transposition.
- Still usually worth reducing if you want stricter pedagogical compliance.

Correction options:
- Change one target note by step to produce a 6th/3rd at the second sonority.
- Introduce contrary or oblique motion in one voice between the two chords.
- Break sequence literalism for one event to avoid the repeated perfect interval.


6) Large Leap (11)
------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- Melodic leaps of 10+ semitones (minor seventh or larger), including octave-plus motions.
- Large skips are harder to sing and often demand compensating stepwise recovery.

Representative report examples:
- m.30 beat 1.00 (Alto): leap of 14 semitones
- m.32 beat 1.00 (Bass): leap of 14 semitones

Why these may be musical anyway:
- Instrumental fugues can support larger leaps than vocal writing.
- Octave transfers can be expressive if balanced by contour and phrase design.

Correction options:
- Replace large leap with two smaller intervals via passing notes.
- If keeping the leap, follow with stepwise motion opposite the leap direction.
- Redistribute octave transfer to another voice where tessitura is more natural.


7) Melodic Tritone (2)
----------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- A single voice leaps by tritone (augmented 4th/diminished 5th), often considered harsh in strict melodic writing.

Representative report examples:
- m.16 beat 1.50: Bass (and Viola in full-score analysis) C# -> G

Why these may be musical anyway:
- In harmonic minor/sequence contexts, tritone outlines can occur idiomatically in instrumental lines.

Correction options:
- Fill the span with passing motion instead of direct leap.
- Redirect one endpoint to nearest chord tone producing a perfect 4th/5th or major/minor 3rd.
- Keep harmonic target but adjust rhythm to approach/leave by step.


8) Direct (Hidden) Fifths (2)
-----------------------------
Why this rule is flagged:
- Outer voices move in similar motion into a perfect fifth, with upper voice leaping.
- This can mimic the effect of parallels even without literal consecutive perfect intervals.

Representative report examples:
- m.16 beat 2.00: Alto & Bass (outer-voice interpretation in current indexing)
- m.32 beat 1.50: Alto & Bass

Why these may be musical anyway:
- In fast contrapuntal textures, some hidden perfects are acceptable if weakly accented and linearly justified.

Correction options:
- Change upper voice approach to stepwise motion.
- Approach target by contrary motion in one outer voice.
- Shift one note to a non-perfect interval at arrival.


Recommended workflow to fix efficiently
---------------------------------------
1. Prioritize structural errors first: Parallel P5ths, Parallel Octaves, Direct 5ths.
2. Next clean tendency-tone handling at cadences: Leading tone unresolved.
3. Then smooth local line shape: Large leaps and Melodic tritones.
4. Treat Parallel unisons and Voice crossings with musical discretion in imitative passages.

5-minute triage pass:
- Focus measures: 7, 9, 16, 21, 32.
- In each, first remove repeated perfect intervals in outer parts.
- Then ensure any accented F# resolves to G unless intentionally sequenced.

End of review.
